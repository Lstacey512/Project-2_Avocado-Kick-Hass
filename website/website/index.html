Originally bred in Southern California, the Hass Avocado is the most popular cultivar of avocados in the world and commands over 80% of total global share. For 2015, the United States alone consumed a staggering 1.66 billion haas avocadosâ€” just over enough for 5 avocados per U.S. resident. But Americans wanted more: to 2017, Hass avocado consumption increased for an annualized growth of 6.1%. 2015 to 2016 in particular showed a sizable spurt in growth (~1.5M in units).

For comparison on the sheer comsuption by year end in 2017: imagine a 747 Boeing airplane. Now imagine 288 of them...and fill them with avocados. Assuming this paces maintains, we would expect just short of 3.4 billion in annual sales within the decade. Holy Guacamole!

Majority of haas avocado sales are between small and large, with extra large only commanding a sliver of sales. The increases in YOY sales trend with greater proportion of small avocados, suggesting that the smaller fruits are contributing the the majority of the growth; between 2015 to 2018, small avocados grow from 48% share to 53% share. The boom in small PLUs could be with associated either with changes in consumer consumption or in supply trends.

Despite the avocado's attraction as a "health halo" food, organic sales are almost insignificant as part of the total sales volume and show only marginal increases in relative share. This could be suggest perceptions that 'organic' is not a function of the health halo, especially as the fruit is encased within a shell (therefore, making it perceived as isolated from pesticides). Or it could be indicative of avocados having a broader appeal beyond a niche "health-focused" audience.

PRICING TRENDS | COLD, HARD HA$$


The average price of avocados is fairly stable with an annualized change of 1.3%. This is despite growing demand volumes, which aligns with typical commodity pricing controls.

Comparing on production methods also shows fairly stable pricing with "organic" status generally commanding only a $0.50 markup.

The below chart breaks out both sales and average price across quarters for the three year period. The relative relationship between price and volume suggests an inverse relationship: when prices are low, volumes are high and vice versa. This again echoes a commodity market; however, a comparison from traditional production to organic does suggest that organic has slightly less price elasticity. Because these volumes vary so drastically, yield uncertainty is most likely the largest contributing factor to price, as we doubt demand factors, cost of inputs or capital would fluctuate nearly as much.

Domestic harvest typically occurs Q2 to Q3 (April through August); in Q3 in particular we begin to see upticks in average price and a subsequent decrease in volume. This persists through Q4, until influx of imported avocados (primarily from Chile) once again reduce prices. We noticed that this fluctuation grows more drastic from 2015 to 2017. Therefore, we wanted to explore if destructive weather during in mid to late-harvest could have impacted yeild during these years. We focused specifically on California and Florida, the primary producers of domestic Haas, and with counties containing major avocado operations highlighted in green. Another study commissioned by the Hass Avocado Board examines the price elasticity of retail sales at a more granular level, and it found that cities with more inelastic demands tended to be correlated with higher per-capita consumption of avocados (Ambrozek, Saitone, & Sexton, 2019). The study also showed that most other Southern California cities were exceptions to Los Angeles and the rest of the nation, possibly suggesting that these areas were the few places in the nation that were not supply-constrained.

ABOUT

This website was developed as Project #2 for a Data Analyis & Visualization bootcamp. Inspiration for this projects came from a dataset sourced from the the Haas Avocado Board and published on Kaggle.com. Supplemental data for analysis was sourced from Aeris Weather API (weather) and US Census API (heat maps). Avocado and census data was processed using Python Pandas library and added to a Postgres SQL database. Visualizations were created using D3, Plotly and Leaflet libraries. Spyscroll and JQuery were also used as additional libraries for website and map functionalities. This website is technically still in development (we have plans to further update the maps to improve functionaly and readibility, as well as refine mobile compatibility), so visit again soon for more!
